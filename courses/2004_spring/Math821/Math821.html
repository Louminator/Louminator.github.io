Course description.

This course focuses on two leading edge methods of computational mathematics for solving challenging fluid flow problems and electromagnetic wave propagation problems.  Half of the course will emphasize particle methods for vorticity dynamics in fluids.  Particle methods capture field equations as a linear combination of moving basis functions.  The other half will emphasize discontinuous Galerkin (DG) methods for a variety of applications and in particular electromagnetic wave propagation.  DG methods represent solutions as an ensemble of possibly discontinuous functions over the domain of interest.  The physical foundations for these problems including the Maxwell equations and the Navier-Stokes equations will be presented for a general technical audience.  To develop robust and accurate schemes, the instructors will develop the rich theoretical foundations of the methods.   In addition to the development and analysis of numerical algorithms, instructors will also introduce topics related to high performance computing such as parallelization and fast summation.  This course will be heavily computational and assessment will be heavily weighted toward student projects.  We hope students will pursue nontrivial applications including flow around bluff bodies or investigations of the performance of the methods on model problems.  Knowledge of a programming language (matlab counts) is required for success in this class.

Projects.

  1. Reproduce Rosenhead's computation of a mixing layer.  Continue his calculation by increasing the problem size and report your findings.  Try different regularizations of the velocity field, including no regularization at all.  Review papers by R. Krasny
    • Krasny, R. Desingularization of periodic vortex sheet roll-up.  J. Comp. Phys.  9 (1986), pp. 292-313.
    • Krasny, R. A study of singularity formation in a vortex sheet by the point vortex approximation.  J. Fluid Mech.  167 (1986), pp. 65-93.
    • Krasny, R.  Vortex sheet computations: roll-up, wake separation in C. Anderson and C. Greengard, eds. Vortex Dynamics and Vortex Methods, vol 28 of Lectures in Applied Mathematics, pp. 385-402.
  2. Calculate the evolution of an inviscid rotating patch of vorticity for several turnover times using different initialization strategies.  Discuss your results as you increase the problem size.  Add diffusion using random walks and compare your results to exact solutions.
  3. Simulate the motion of an inviscid vortex dipole inside a square domain with solid boundaries.
  4. Calculate the viscous flow around a square cylinder.
  5. Compare inviscid and viscous collisions of two dipoles.

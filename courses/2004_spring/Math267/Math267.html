Course description.

Rather than covering a new mathematical subject, this course focuses on helping students see the ever elusive Big Picture.  That is, we are designing this course to help you understand what you do with mathematics and where it fits into your life, your career, our society, other societies, other planets, other geometries, other universes and so on.  The course is flexible in nature and we hope those who enroll will participate actively.  Perspective on Mathematics is supposed to benefit all students rather than burden them, so it only meets on Wednesday mornings for a single unit.  Meeting content will vary, but all meetings will include time for student discussion.  Most of all, I am looking forward to learning from you to make this the best course possible.  The course material will evolve with emerging student interests.

By the way, what is the thing in the upper left corner of this page next to the course title?  This is a fountain outside the Sydney Convention Center.  When my colleague Toby Driscoll and I traveled to Australia this summer to attend the International Congress of Industrial and Applied Mathematicians, we were both fascinated by this fountains.  Toby snapped this digital image from a second story balcony.  You can click on the picture to get a full view.  Sometimes, kids would play in it even though it was July which is the middle of winter Down Under.  Mathematicians see things differently though, and Toby and I got into a rather involved discussion of whether or not the wave crests formed a rectilinear or hexagonal pattern on the surface.  It was hard to tell because the curving walls complicate things a bit.  Well, I played around a bit with some image manipulation software, and while this is not conclusive, it looks like one can a hexagonal tesselation.  Below, I show the original photo, the enhancement and then what I see in the enhancement.

Anyway, part of doing mathematics is looking at things from different perspectives.  We are trying to apply this principle to this new course.

Course topics.

Course topics may include some of the following:
  • Research presentations from faculty who are interested in working with students during the summer or during the academic year.
  • Presentations by junior and senior math majors who are involved in research collaborations with faculty members.
  • Presentations on the teaching of mathematics at all levels.
  • A tour of the MEC lab.
  • An overview of the library research facilities.
  • Discussion of writing in the mathematical sciences.
  • A presentation on scientific computation in the mathematical sciences.
  • Some topics on mathematics and world history.
  • Perspectives on international mathematical collaborations.
  • Student contests and regional activities.

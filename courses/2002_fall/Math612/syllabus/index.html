Course description: This course explores a variety of numerical techniques for approximating solution to partial differential equations. All techniques to a greater or lesser extent are problem dependent. This course will include on a menu of classic partial differential equations including diffusion equations, wave equations, Burger's equation and convection-diffusion equations.


Prerequisites: MATH503 or MEEG863 or PHYS207.


Your objectives:

By the end of this course, every passing student should ...

  • ... have a thorough understanding of the accuracy and stability of multistep and Runge-Kutta methods for ordinary differential equations.

  • ... understand how to use finite differences to implement the method of lines for partial differential equations.

  • ... understand how to analyze the consistency and stability of finite difference methods for simple linear partial differential equations such as the heat equation.

  • ... have a thorough understanding of explicit, implicit, Crank-Nicholson, leap-frog and ADI methods for diffusion problems.

  • ... be able to use energy estimates and Fourier stability analysis to study finite difference methods.

  • ... understand the CFL condition for numerical schemes and its import in choosing numerical parameters.

  • ... know the Lax Equivalence Theorem and be able to apply it to finite difference schemes.

  • ... understand boundary value problems for ordinary differential equations.

  • ... understand and be able to implement fast Poisson solvers.

  • ... be familiar with vortex methods for incompressible flows.

  • ... be familiar with multigrid methods for dissipative problems.

  • ... be familiar with spectral schemes for periodic problems.

  • ... be able to implement any of the methods discussed in this course.

Computing projects: Computing assignments play a central role in this course. One must practice mathematics to master it, and mathematical computation is no exception. I expect your projects to be presented as well-crafted reports.

  • Your intended audience is a someone with the same level of understanding as yourself or better.

  • The report should state the problem, the method of solution, the solution and some discussion of its range of applicability. A strong report should make a concise and thorough connection between the problem and the solution.

  • You should give the reader a convincing reason to believe your solution. Some form of error analysis or refinement study should be a part of your argument.

Tentative list of topics:


  • Multistep and Runge-Kutta schemes for ordinary differential equations.

  • Consistency, stability and convergence of numerical methods for systems of ordinary differential equations.

  • Finite differences.

  • Method of lines.

  • Explicit, implicit, Crank-Nicholson, leap-frog and alternative direction implicit (ADI) schemes for diffusive problems.

  • Energy estimates.

  • Fourier stability analysis.

  • CFL conditions.

  • Lax Equivalence Theorem.

  • Boundary value problems.

  • Fast Poisson solvers.

  • Spectral methods. Fast Fourier transforms.

  • Multigrid methods.

  • Vortex methods for incompressible flows.
